## Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning
### Title and Authors
The paper "Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning" is written by Shihao Gu, Bryan Kelly, and Dacheng Xiu.

### Research Question
The research question of this paper is to investigate the effectiveness of [[Machine Learning]] methods in measuring asset risk premia, a fundamental problem in [[Empirical Asset Pricing]]. The authors aim to compare the performance of different machine learning methods in predicting stock returns and identifying the most informative predictor variables.

### Methodology
The authors use a variety of [[Machine Learning]] methods, including [[Neural Networks]], [[Regression Trees]], and [[Generalized Linear Models]], to predict stock returns. They also employ [[Regularization]] techniques to prevent [[Overfitting]] and ensure stable out-of-sample performance. The authors use a large dataset of stock-level predictive characteristics and macroeconomic predictors to train and test their models.

### Key Findings
The authors find that machine learning methods can significantly improve the prediction of stock returns, with some methods doubling the performance of leading regression-based strategies. Specifically, they report:
* A portfolio strategy that times the S&P 500 with neural network forecasts enjoys an annualized out-of-sample [[Sharpe Ratio]] of 0.77, versus the 0.51 Sharpe ratio of a buy-and-hold investor.
* A value-weighted long-short decile spread strategy that takes positions based on stock-level neural network forecasts earns an annualized out-of-sample Sharpe ratio of 1.35, more than doubling the performance of a leading regression-based strategy from the literature.

### Data Sources Used
The authors use a large dataset of stock-level predictive characteristics and macroeconomic predictors, including:
* Stock-level predictive signals, such as [[Momentum]], [[Liquidity]], and [[Volatility]]
* Macroeconomic predictors, such as [[GDP]] and [[Inflation]]

### Policy Implications
The authors' findings have significant implications for investors and policymakers. The use of machine learning methods can help investors make more informed decisions and improve their portfolio performance. Additionally, the identification of the most informative predictor variables can help policymakers better understand the underlying drivers of asset prices and make more informed decisions about monetary policy.

### Connections to Other Research
This paper contributes to the growing literature on the application of [[Machine Learning]] in [[Finance]] and [[Economics]]. The authors' findings are related to other research on the use of machine learning in [[Asset Pricing]], such as the work of Gu et al. (2019) and Kelly et al. (2019). The paper also builds on the literature on [[Empirical Asset Pricing]], including the work of Green et al. (2013) and Harvey et al. (2016).
